Objective Benchmarking of Vietnamese Zero-Shot Voice Cloning: F5-TTS ViVoice Against Five Text-to-Speech Baselines

Group 4 · Data Science Capstone Project (DSP391m)

FPT University — Summer 2026 · Corresponding contact: group4reldsp@gmail.com

Abstract

Voice cloning text-to-speech (TTS) must satisfy two competing objectives at once: the synthesized speech must be intelligible (it says the right words) and must preserve speaker identity (it sounds like the reference voice). We present a reproducible, objective benchmark of six TTS systems on Vietnamese, centered on F5-TTS ViVoice, a Diffusion-Transformer model with a Conditional Flow-Matching objective and a Vocos 24 kHz vocoder, evaluated against XTTS-v2, MMS-TTS, Piper, Edge-TTS, and Bark.

Because generative speech has no single ground-truth label, we quantify quality with automatic proxies: Word/Character Error Rate (WER/CER) obtained by an automatic-speech-recognition (ASR) round-trip through Whisper, speaker similarity (SECS) via cosine distance over Resemblyzer embeddings, and generation speed via the real-time factor (RTF). Under a fixed sentence, seed, and reference sample, F5-TTS attains the lowest WER (5.9%) while remaining in the top tier of speaker similarity (0.837) — the only engine that jointly optimizes both axes. We report the full trade-off, discuss where each engine belongs, and state the study's limitations candidly, including a pilot-scale test set and the fact that the fine-tuning pipeline is hardened but not yet verified end-to-end on real speaker data.

Index Terms — Vietnamese TTS, zero-shot voice cloning, flow matching, diffusion transformer, objective evaluation, WER, speaker similarity.

1 Introduction

Zero-shot voice cloning takes a short reference recording of an unseen speaker and a target text, and reads that text in the reference speaker's voice — without any per-speaker training. It underpins accessibility tools, dubbing, and personalized assistants, but Vietnamese remains under-served relative to English: the language's six lexical tones and dense diacritics make both synthesis and its evaluation harder, and a mispronounced tone can change word meaning entirely.

This report addresses the Model & Results stage of the capstone. Our contributions are: (i) a system built around F5-TTS ViVoice with a corrected, library-faithful inference pipeline and a production-hardened fine-tuning pipeline; (ii) an objective, reproducible evaluation harness that turns the vague notion of "sounds good" into measurable quantities (WER, CER, SECS, RTF, and audio-signal metrics) computable fully offline; and (iii) a head-to-head benchmark of six engines under identical conditions, with an explicit analysis of the intelligibility–identity–speed trade-off and an honest account of what the numbers can and cannot support.

2 Background and Related Work

Modern non-autoregressive TTS has converged on transformer backbones paired with generative objectives. F5-TTS [1] uses a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) [3] trained with a Conditional Flow-Matching (CFM) objective [2], which learns a continuous vector field transporting noise to the target mel-spectrogram distribution; a Vocos neural vocoder [4] then converts mel-spectrograms to a 24 kHz waveform. The Vietnamese checkpoint hynt/F5-TTS-Vietnamese-ViVoice [10] is pre-trained on roughly one thousand hours of Vietnamese speech.

For comparison we include XTTS-v2 [5], a competing multilingual zero-shot cloning model; MMS-TTS [6], Meta's single-speaker Vietnamese VITS; Piper [9], a fast local ONNX voice; Edge-TTS, Microsoft's cloud neural voice used as a high-quality single-speaker baseline; and Bark [8], included as a deliberately weak lower bound because it has no Vietnamese support. Evaluation relies on Whisper [7] for the ASR round-trip and on a GE2E-style speaker encoder [11] (Resemblyzer) for identity similarity.

3 System and Model Architecture

3.1 Model selection

F5-TTS ViVoice was selected as the primary model because it uniquely combines zero-shot cloning (a short reference suffices, with no per-speaker fine-tuning), strong Vietnamese pre-training, a state-of-the-art DiT+CFM backbone that avoids the monotone quality of single-speaker VITS, and a research-permissive license (CC-BY-NC-SA-4.0) suitable for a non-commercial academic project. The five remaining engines were chosen to span the trade-off space, from competing cloning (XTTS-v2) to fast fixed-voice TTS (MMS, Piper, Edge) to a weak floor (Bark).

3.2 Architecture

The architecture is loaded verbatim from the library's F5TTS_Base.yaml so that it matches the pre-trained checkpoint exactly:

CFM (Conditional Flow-Matching) wrapper
  └─ DiT backbone: dim=1024, depth=22, heads=16, ff_mult=2,
                   text_dim=512, conv_layers=4,
                   text_mask_padding=false,  pe_attn_head=1   ← compatibility-critical
  └─ Vocoder: Vocos @ 24 kHz (mel → waveform)
Parameters ≈ 336M · Audio front-end: n_fft=1024, hop=256, win=1024, n_mel=100
Compatibility invariant

The two fields text_mask_padding=false and pe_attn_head=1 must not be altered. When omitted, DiT falls back to v1 defaults and the forward pass silently mis-computes despite correctly-shaped weights, degrading output to a near-monotone signal (spectral flatness collapses to ≈0.0019). Restoring the exact architecture returns spectral flatness to the human-speech range (≈0.11–0.14). This was one of the two principal defects diagnosed and fixed during development.

3.3 Inference pipeline

The F5-TTS path uses the library's official preprocess_ref_audio_text and infer_process utilities so that tokenization (convert_char_to_pinyin) and preprocessing match training. Long inputs are normalized (Unicode NFC, diacritics preserved), split at sentence/clause boundaries with URLs, e-mails, decimals, dates, and abbreviations protected from splitting, synthesized chunk-by-chunk under a fixed seed, joined with 180 ms of silence, and peak-normalized (≤0.99) to prevent clipping. Each engine lazy-loads and caches independently; an RLock plus a concurrency-1 queue prevents out-of-memory from concurrent model loads, and the system falls back to CPU if CUDA/MPS fails.

4 Data and Training Methodology

The fine-tuning objective is single-speaker adaptation while avoiding catastrophic forgetting of the pre-trained model. The pipeline (scripts/train.py) proceeds from a metadata manifest through a deterministic train/validation split, a padding-aware collate function, the CFM flow-matching loss, mixed-precision back-propagation with gradient accumulation, gradient clipping, and a manual warm-up-then-cosine learning-rate schedule; the best checkpoint is selected by validation loss.

The pipeline is hardened for reproducibility and provenance: exponential-moving-average weights are loaded correctly from the official checkpoint; the train/validation split is deterministic under seed 42; NaN/Inf losses abort immediately; resumption is refused if metadata is missing or the base model mismatches; each checkpoint stores a JSON record of base model, architecture, vocabulary size, config, git commit, step, validation loss, and dataset summary.

Honest status

The fine-tuning pipeline is hardened and unit-tested at the component level, but not yet verified end-to-end on real human-speaker data with a GPU; the blocker is the absence of 30–60 minutes of clean, hand-transcribed Vietnamese speech. Earlier checkpoints trained on synthetic tones were quarantined and are not used to claim quality. Accordingly, the results in §6 characterize the zero-shot base model, not a fine-tuned one.

5 Evaluation Methodology

5.1 Metrics

Generative speech lacks a simple correctness label, so quality is measured through objective proxies on two core axes plus speed. Intelligibility is measured by WER and CER via an ASR round-trip: Whisper (small, beam 5, language=vi) re-transcribes the generated audio, and the transcript is compared to the reference text with edit distance (via jiwer, with an equivalent local fallback). Vietnamese diacritics are preserved by default. Speaker identity is measured by SECS: the cosine similarity between Resemblyzer embeddings of the generated and reference audio. Speed is the real-time factor, RTF = inference time ÷ audio duration. Auxiliary signal metrics (RMS, peak, clipping ratio, spectral flatness) screen for degenerate output.

Interpretation guardrails

SECS is a relative similarity score, not biometric proof of identity. WER/CER are upper bounds on true error because Whisper itself errs, especially on tones — so the numbers are most meaningful relatively, when comparing engines under identical inputs, rather than as absolute rates.

5.2 Experimental setup

All six engines synthesize the same sentence — "Trí tuệ nhân tạo đang thay đổi cách con người giao tiếp và làm việc mỗi ngày." (17 words) — from the same reference sample under a fixed seed (42). The harness is fail-soft: each case carries its own status, a corrupt file or missing dependency does not abort the batch, and exit codes distinguish full success, total failure, and an invalid manifest. Cross-validation in the classical k-fold sense is impractical for fine-tuning a 336M-parameter generative model on tens of minutes of audio; its role is filled by a deterministic held-out validation loss during training and an independent fixed test set of diverse sentences (short, numeric, decimal, date, abbreviation, proper-noun, interrogative, compound, and long) for post-hoc metric evaluation.

7 Discussion and Insights

F5-TTS wins on balance. It is the only engine that is simultaneously the most intelligible (WER 5.9%) and near the top on identity (SECS 0.837) — precisely the joint objective of voice cloning. XTTS-v2 is a strong, faster alternative: the highest SECS (0.888) and roughly 5× faster than F5-TTS on CPU, but with double the WER — the right choice when identity and speed outweigh accuracy.

Fixed-voice TTS is not cloning. MMS, Piper, and Edge are intelligible and very fast (RTF < 1) yet score SECS≈0.6, confirming they do not reproduce the reference speaker; they suit applications that only need clear Vietnamese. CER stays below WER for every good engine — F5-TTS errs chiefly on the sentence-initial word ("Trí"→"Chí", a tr/ch onset merge common in regional pronunciation), so CER (2.6%) is far below WER (5.9%): a dialectal pronunciation artifact, not an intelligibility failure. Finally, the metrics catch real failure: Bark is automatically flagged (WER > 100%, lowest SECS), demonstrating that the harness genuinely separates broken from working systems.

8 Limitations and Threats to Validity

  • Pilot-scale test (n = 1 sentence/engine). This is an audit-scale study; it does not yet support confidence intervals or standard deviations. A ≥12-sentence diverse set is prepared for the follow-up.
  • ASR is itself imperfect. Whisper mis-transcribes, particularly tones, so WER/CER are upper bounds; read them comparatively.
  • SECS is relative, not biometric verification.
  • Speed measured on CPU and inclusive of first-load overhead for the first engine; absolute RTF will differ substantially on GPU.
  • Base model only. Results characterize the zero-shot model; fine-tuning has not been verified end-to-end, so no fine-tuning improvement is claimed.

9 Conclusion and Future Work

We built and objectively benchmarked a Vietnamese zero-shot voice-cloning system. F5-TTS ViVoice is the right primary model: it leads on intelligibility and ranks in the top tier on speaker identity, the only engine to optimize both axes at once. Equally important, the project delivers a reproducible objective-evaluation framework that converts subjective impressions into verifiable measurements and that surfaced two subtle, high-impact defects during development.

Future work, in priority order: (1) scale the test set from one to ≥12 diverse sentences for mean ± standard-deviation reporting; (2) collect 30–60 minutes of real speaker audio and run fine-tuning end-to-end on GPU, measuring WER/CER/SECS before versus after adaptation; (3) add subjective Mean-Opinion-Score listening tests alongside the objective metrics; (4) optimize F5-TTS latency on GPU, including controlled NFE reduction; and (5) package the system (Docker / Hugging Face Spaces) for setup-free demonstration.
